Lesson 0001

Agentic AI Is a Control Loop

Tangible win: classify a system as predictive, generative, or agentic, then map one agentic workflow into perceive, interpret, reason, act, observe, and update steps.

The One Idea

Predictive AI maps inputs to estimates. Generative AI maps prompts or context to artifacts. Agentic AI adds a closed loop: it observes an environment, reasons against a goal or condition, acts through a controlled interface, observes the result, and continues or stops. The local architecture notes define this as the practical difference between a one-shot model call and an agentic system, grounded in the classic agent-environment framing from AIMA.

The three categories are not rivals. Predictive and generative models can be components inside an agentic system — they show up in perception, reasoning, planning, memory, tool selection, or monitoring. The churn-risk scorer and the reply-drafter from the quiz below are not agentic on their own, but either could be a tool the loop calls. What makes the system agentic is that the architecture is organized around action in an environment, not the presence or absence of any one model.

Keep the compact version open: Agentic Control Loop Reference.

The Minimum Test

A system is agentic only when all three are explicit: an environment it can observe, an action surface that can change that environment, and a feedback path that decides whether to continue, recover, escalate, or stop. If any one is missing, you probably have a model call, workflow, or tool-backed response rather than an agent architecture.

Recall Check

Hide the text above and answer from memory in one sentence: what is the minimum that makes a system agentic rather than generative?

It runs a closed loop that observes an environment, acts through a controlled interface against a goal or rule, observes the result, and continues, recovers, escalates, or stops — generation alone produces an artifact and then ends.

Design Drill

Map this workflow into the loop from memory:

Workflow: a calendar assistant receives a meeting request, checks availability, proposes times, books the meeting after approval, and watches for conflicts.

Perceive: meeting request, attendees, calendar state. Interpret: required duration, constraints, conflicts. Reason: choose candidate slots within policy. Act: propose times, then book only after approval. Observe: booking success, declines, new conflicts. Update or stop: record task state, retry proposals, escalate, or stop when confirmed.

Self-check: the environment is the calendar system plus attendee responses; the environment-changing action is booking; approval gates the externally visible action; the stop condition is a confirmed meeting or escalation.

Design Drill 2

Different domain, same loop. Map this one from memory and notice which step carries the irreversible action:

Workflow: an on-call agent watches alerts, correlates them to a likely failing service, drains traffic from a bad node after approval, then verifies error rates recover or rolls back.

Perceive: alerts, metrics, deploy events, node health. Interpret: which service is degraded and whether alerts share a root cause. Reason: pick the smallest safe remediation within runbook policy. Act: drain the bad node — an irreversible, externally visible action that needs approval. Observe: error rate, latency, downstream effects. Update or stop: confirm recovery and stop, retry on another node, roll back, or escalate to a human.

Self-check: the risky action is not "reasoning about the alert"; it is changing production traffic. That boundary is where policy, approval, rollback, and audit belong.

Design Drill 3: Rewrite the Vague Brief

Most "AI agent" pitches hide their autonomy. Rewrite this one-line brief into precise boundaries: what it observes, which actions can change the environment, what limits and approvals constrain those actions, and what stop condition ends the loop.

Vague brief: "An AI agent that handles customer refunds automatically."

Observes: the refund request, order and payment history, return status, fraud signals, and refund policy. Environment-changing actions: issuing the refund to the payment processor — irreversible and externally visible — plus notifying the customer and updating the case. Limits & approvals: auto-refund only within a dollar cap and policy window; anything above the cap, outside policy, or flagged for fraud routes to a human. Stop condition: refund confirmed and case closed, escalation handed off, or request denied with reason recorded. Notice that "automatically" hid an irreversible money-moving action that needs an enforced cap and an approval path.

Self-check: if your rewrite does not name the money-moving interface, the approval boundary, and the stop condition, the autonomy is still underspecified.

Architecture Check

Before calling a system agentic, ask:

  • What environment does it observe and affect?
  • Which actions change that environment?
  • What feedback arrives after each action?
  • What approval, rollback, audit, or stop condition controls unsafe action?
